## Part 1 — What CareerPilot is

CareerPilot is a web app that helps non-technical professionals build AI
skills for the changing shape of white-collar work. The working analogy is a
**fitness app for your career**: an honest assessment of where you stand, a
personalized plan to improve, and daily tracking that keeps you moving.

**It is curriculum-led.** This is the central point for this review. Many
products will tell you how exposed your job is to AI — that part is close to a
commodity. CareerPilot's actual product is what comes *after* the assessment:
a personalized, **in-house** AI-skills learning curriculum. The assessment is
the intake and diagnostic; the curriculum is the thing of value, the thing
users return to.

**How the curriculum works — "the buffet."** Behind the scenes, CareerPilot
maintains a curated library of short skill units — the buffet. When a user
finishes onboarding (background + a short questionnaire + the exposure
assessment), an AI "selector" marks up that library *for them*: some units
tagged core, some "when you have time," some skipped — each with a one-line
reason explaining how it helps that specific person reach their goal. The user
sees this marked-up menu, can adjust it, commits to it, and then works through
it with progress tracking. The personalization is curation made visible, not a
curriculum invented from scratch.

**Unit 01, the subject of this review, is one unit from that buffet** — the
foundational one. The buffet does not exist yet; Unit 01 is being built first,
as the template the rest of the library will follow.

**Design commitments that shape every unit:**
- Units are short — 15 to 30 minutes, completable in one sitting. The bar is
  that a unit fits into a real workday, in the gap where a person would
  otherwise scroll social media.
- The curriculum's v1 focus is helping a user get **better, faster, and more
  valuable in the job they already have** — not changing careers. (Career
  change is a planned later expansion.)
- The plan paces to the user's stated weekly time and re-paces if they slow
  down. CareerPilot never shows a user as "behind" — it re-times.
- Tone is constructive and calm, never alarmist. Exposure is framed as a
  starting point, not a verdict.
- Learning content is **tool-neutral**: a unit teaches a skill, then names
  best-in-class tools as interchangeable options, without pushing any vendor.

**Users.** Three profiles: experienced professionals (50s+), mid-career
professionals whose roles are being reshaped by AI (35–45), and people early
in their careers (22–28). The MVP is tuned for the mid-career profile.

**Business model and stage.** A free tier (assessment plus a plan preview), a
paid individual tier (the full curriculum and tracking), and later tiers for
small companies and large enterprises. The first tiers are bootstrapped; the
enterprise tier is the funded phase. The product is currently in active
development — the assessment and onboarding flow are built; the curriculum is
the next major piece.

## Part 2 — The competitive landscape

CareerPilot sits near three groups of existing products. Understanding them is
necessary to judge whether Unit 01 is differentiated enough to matter.

**1. Free AI job-risk calculators.** A crowded category — willaireplaceme.app,
willaireplaceme.io, whataboutai, careeragents, inspireambitions, replacemeter,
and others. They take a job title or a short task description and return an
automation-risk score, usually with a breakdown of exposed versus resilient
tasks and some generic advice. They are free, instant, and built to be shared
on social media. Critically: they are **one-shot diagnostics**. For the actual
learning, they refer users *out* — to Udemy, DataCamp, Coursera, and similar
generic course platforms.

**2. Consumer AI career coaches.** Conversational tools that give advice. They
lack a structured, trackable plan — the interaction ends when the conversation
does.

**3. Enterprise career-pathing tools.** Products like Fuel50 and TalentGuard.
Strong structured planning and tracking, but they live inside large employers
and serve internal mobility only — not open to an independent professional.

**The gap CareerPilot targets.** No one serves the independent professional
with a structured, trackable, *in-house* learning curriculum. The calculators
stop at the score and outsource the learning. The coaches do not structure.
The enterprise tools are not open to individuals.

**Why this matters for reviewing Unit 01.** CareerPilot's strategic bet is
that the exposure score is a commodity, and the **learning delivery is the
differentiator** — short, concrete, hands-on, in-house micro-units personalized
to the user, instead of a referral to a generic multi-hour course. Unit 01 is
a direct sample of that learning delivery. Reviewing Unit 01 is therefore
reviewing the core of what is supposed to make CareerPilot defensible. The key
question running underneath the whole review: *is this genuinely better than
sending the user to a free YouTube video or a Udemy module?*

## Part 3 — What you're reviewing: Buffet Unit 01

### 3.1 — The buffet unit structure (the template)

Every unit in the curriculum follows this structure. Unit 01 is also the test
of whether this structure works as a repeatable template for a 10–12 unit
library.

| Field | What it holds |
|---|---|
| Title | Short, plain, inviting |
| Skill | One line: what the user can DO after the unit |
| Time | Total minutes, target 15–30 |
| Tier | Foundation / Applied / Build |
| Prerequisites | Earlier units that should come first, or none |
| Why this matters | Why the unit is worth the time, in work terms |
| Teaching | The content — 3 to 6 concrete ideas, kept short |
| Exercise | One hands-on task, done in a real tool, ~15 minutes |
| Success check | How the user knows it worked — a self-check |
| Tools | Best-in-class options, tool-neutral |
| Going deeper | Optional pointer for "when you have time" |

### 3.2 — Unit 01, in full

```
Title: Working with AI assistants well
Skill: Get genuinely useful output from an AI assistant by briefing it
       properly — instead of vague questions and mediocre answers.
Time: 25 minutes (about 8 minutes reading, ~15 minutes exercise)
Tier: Foundation
Prerequisites: none — this is the starting point
```

**Why this matters for your work**

Most professionals use an AI assistant like a search box: one quick, vague
question, a mediocre answer, and a quiet conclusion that AI is overhyped. The
people getting real value do something different — they brief the AI the way
they would brief a capable new colleague. Same tool, completely different
results.

The gap between those two is not intelligence, seniority, or technical skill.
It is knowing how to ask. This is the most leveraged 25 minutes in the whole
curriculum: every other AI skill — writing, analysis, automation — depends on
being able to communicate with the tool.

**The teaching — five things that change your output**

1. *Context is everything.* The AI knows nothing about your situation, your
company, your audience, or your goal unless you tell it. A vague request gets
a generic answer. Before the request, state who it is for, what you are trying
to achieve, and any constraints that matter.

2. *Say what "good" looks like.* "Write a project update" could mean anything.
"Write a 150-word project update for my manager — factual tone, lead with
status, then flag the two risks" gives the AI a target. Specify format,
length, tone, and audience.

3. *Show an example when you have one.* If you have a past email or a document
in the style you want, paste it in and say "match this." AI is exceptionally
good at matching a pattern — one good example often beats three paragraphs of
instructions.

4. *Treat it as a conversation, not a vending machine.* The first answer is a
draft. The real value is in the next two turns: "make it shorter," "too
formal," "you missed the budget point." People who get great results are
iterating quickly, not writing one perfect prompt.

5. *Give it a role.* Starting with "You are an experienced [financial analyst
/ recruiter / editor]" measurably shifts the output toward that perspective.

**The exercise — the same task, two ways (~15 minutes)**

Pick one real task from your actual work this week — a real email, a summary,
a piece of analysis. Not a made-up task; something you genuinely have to do.

*Round 1 — the way you would normally do it.* Open your AI assistant and
prompt it the quick way: a short, vague request. Read the result. Keep it.

*Round 2 — brief it properly.* Start a fresh conversation. Give it a role,
give it context, say what good looks like, paste an example if you have one.
Then read the first draft and iterate at least twice until it is genuinely
usable.

Put the two outputs side by side.

**How you will know it worked**

Two signs. First: the Round 2 output is something you could use with light
editing, while Round 1 would need a real rewrite. Second, and more important:
you can name the specific things you added in Round 2 that made the
difference. If you can articulate them, you can repeat them tomorrow.

**Tools for this unit**

This skill works the same across all major AI assistants — use whichever you
already have: ChatGPT (OpenAI), Claude (Anthropic), Gemini (Google), Microsoft
Copilot. A paid plan is not needed for this exercise. The skill transfers
across all of them.

**Going deeper (optional)**

The systematic version of this — repeatable prompt structures, deliberate use
of examples and step-by-step reasoning — is Unit 02, Structured prompting.

## Part 4 — The review brief

We are engaging you to review one learning unit from CareerPilot's curriculum.
Please treat this as a professional review engagement and respond accordingly.

### Engagement context

CareerPilot's curriculum is its core differentiator (see Parts 1 and 2). Before
the remaining nine to eleven units are built, we want a rigorous review of the
first one. Unit 01 is being assessed in two capacities at once: as a standalone
learning unit a user will actually complete, and as the template every later
unit will follow. A weakness in the template propagates across the whole
library, so structural critique is as valuable as content critique.

### What we need assessed

Please give us your assessment across these six areas. Be specific. Where you
identify a problem, explain why it is a problem and what you would change —
but do not rewrite the unit; we want your judgment, so we can decide.

**A. Instructional design.** Is this effective micro-learning? Is the structure
— why it matters, teaching, exercise, success check — sound? Does the exercise
genuinely teach the skill through practice, or does it mostly describe it? Is
25 minutes a realistic estimate for the content as written?

**B. Content quality and currency.** Is the teaching accurate and current as
of today? Is anything important about this skill missing? Is anything included
that is not worth the space in a 25-minute unit? Are the four named tools the
right best-in-class set, and is the tool-neutral framing handled well?

**C. Audience fit.** The target user is a non-technical mid-career
professional — competent with everyday software, but a beginner at deliberate,
skilled use of AI. Is the unit pitched correctly for that person — not
condescending, not academic, not assuming prior knowledge it should not?

**D. Strategic fit and differentiation.** This is the most important question.
CareerPilot's premise is that in-house micro-learning beats referring users to
generic courses. Does Unit 01 deliver on that? Is it meaningfully better than
what the user could get free from a YouTube video or a Udemy module on
prompting? If it is not clearly better, say so plainly.

**E. Scalability as a template.** Does this unit's structure work as a model
for a 10–12 unit library? What would break, get repetitive, or feel formulaic
if this exact template were applied across ten more units? What should change
in the template now, before the rest are built?

**F. Risks and the single biggest weakness.** What is the weakest part of this
unit? What is most likely to make a user abandon it partway, or finish it
unconvinced? If you could fix only one thing, what would it be?

### Deliverable

A prioritized set of specific, critical notes organized by the six areas
above, ending with the single change you would make first. We are looking for
gaps and honest weaknesses, not reassurance — a review that only confirms our
choices is not useful to us. Direct criticism is welcome and expected.
